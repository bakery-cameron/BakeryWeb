THE FUTURE OF THE FACEBOOK ADVERTISEMENT

All Articles

Next

Previous

Facebook has reported that it is now looking in to sandwiching fifteen-second television style ads in between the status updates of users’ newsfeed. We all saw this coming. It is the logical conclusion. Facebook already has us familiar with the concept of an easily digestible fifteen-second video through its application Instagram. It also already has information regarding your gender, age, likes, and sometimes even geographical ‘check-in’ data. They have great data that they have the ability to in to great targeting potential. And they are likely to use it in ways less subtle than what we are used to.

Case in point, currently after you look up Blendtec blenders on amazon, your Facebook’s right sidebar ‘Marketplace Ads’ will often seem oddly intuitive and helpful, suggesting local smoothie shops. Subtle. Perhaps you saw this as a happy coincidence. “Oh, how nice!” “Facebook knows I am getting married and is showing me vacation spots in Branson, Missouri. Its like Facebook knows me!” It does, and it is able to sell this knowledge to marketers wishing to increase awareness of their products.

Essentially, this new format of highly trafficked ad space creates a new competitive arena for media buying. Social network analytics page Mashable, identified that during Miley Cyrus’ VMA performance 90% of viewers interacted (comment, like, post) on Facebook, creating 26.5 million unique interactions about Miley Cyrus. This means that not only are viewers just watching a program on television, but interacting further with second screen options. The possibility to highly target based on a scheduled event (Oscars, VMAs, Sports Events) and demographics are now possible.

What does this mean? Have we reached a new age of hyper targeted ads that speak specifically to the intended viewer? Perhaps, but not likely considering the price tag Facebook has attached to its new media slots. Prices will range from $1 million to $2.5 million to run an ad for a single day. This kind of exposure will likely only be used by big name established brands, and I estimate used only as a second screen option fitting in to larger campaigns.

Keep an eye out for these ads. It will be interesting to see how the video will be used within an online framework. The 15-second time limit allows for a lot of creative freedom and hopefully advertisers won’t fall in to hackneyed tropes common to television. I want to see more Muscle Music, more off beat is-it, isn’t-it, an ad, and better creative overall. Of course, if not done well it could be very annoying; like a video of guy trying to sell vacations to Branson, Missouri.
