Design the ExperienceNot the Layout

All Articles

Next

Previous

The designer’s struggle to stay relevant, as browsers push forward and web standard progress, will be the driving force in crafting better responsive layouts and user experience. A user experience is a result of many aspects of the product. These include, (but are not limited to), an array of animations: from transitional, on load, on scroll, and even movement using a device’s accelerometer. Of course not every project demands bells and whistles but, as technology progresses, so to will the average user’s expectations.

This trend has forced a broadening in many designers’ skill set beyond simple HTML and CSS. Today, a strong tool set includes CSS3 animations and filters, Javascript, and motion graphics applications. Diving into these realms has drastically altered the way in which we perceive and design interfaces. For a designer to arrive at any solution, knowing your options is a key step and without knowing “what’s under the hood”, so to speak, no one can make a truly informed decision.

While CSS3 animations are very powerful, in order to gain full control over the elements on the page and the experience in it’s entirety, javascript is a must. I'm not suggesting everyone spend the next three years devoting themselves to coding; Even just the basics will expand any designer’s understanding of exactly what’s possible on the web. This addition would not only help in the design phase but, communicating with developers will no longer take any translation. This improvement on communication will insure what is in your head ends up on the screen.

When mocking-up animations are required, either for client approval or development reference, I use Adobe After Effects to showcase space and timing. Here is an animation I made for the bakery’s homepage slider.

While laying out a design, one should always ask oneselves “what is happening”? Is the site going to be static? If not, how does it move? How does it behave? Above you’ll see a simple mock-up made in After Effects which depicts how we wanted the homepage slider to behave. If a web designer wanted this particular timing and effect, a video example Is the best option, second only to coding the product themselves. Too often the segregation between design and dev forces developers to interpret intentions.

Some say pick a field and master it. I argue learning tangent skills widens not only your skill set but your understanding of your craft. Much like a Chef learning where his ingredients are from, to produce a well thought-out product every digital designer should know what’s going on under the hood.

Cameron Miller
