Marketing and The New Minority

All Articles

Next

Previous

While most marketing and ad folk likely had “TGIF” (the sentiment; not ABC Family’s hit programming series) on their minds as they awaited the end of the workday on August 22nd, the multicultural marketing consultancy, EthniFacts, was counting down to the end of the day for a different reason -- the tipping point at which the segment of the population that identify themselves as minorities would become the majority. EthniFacts had a countdown running on their site, America Reimagined, to mark this momentous occasion.

Here at Bakery, we were watching the countdown closely, as the shift in American demographics impacts everyone at the agency on both personal and professional levels. Bakery is a minority-owned advertising agency with a diverse portfolio of work that includes and offers strategic projects for brands aiming to connect with multicultural consumers.

Bakery is comprised of team members who hail from the Dominican Republic, Iran, Costa Rica and all over the United States. We’re a tight-knit group of immigrants and first, second, third, and fourth generation Americans, and we each carry with us cultural identities that influence our affinities and tendencies. This perspective along with informed research on the target audiences of the campaigns and products we produce allows us to create user- centric work that encompasses human factors, language styles, imagery, and concepts that resonate with the people we intend to reach.

Statistics abound around the shifting composition of the American population. According to the US Census:

  • About 353 of the nation's 3,143 counties, or 11 percent, are now "majority-minority." Six of those counties tipped to that status last year: Mecklenburg, N.C.; Cherokee, Okla.; Texas, Okla.; Bell, Texas; Hockley, Texas; and Terrell, Texas.
  • In 2012, 13 states and the District of Columbia had an under-5 age population that was "majority-minority," up from five states in 2000. In 25 states and the District of Columbia, minorities now make up more than 40 percent of the under-5 group.

      • This changing landscape is an exciting frontier for the country. Previously under-represented groups will command thoughtful attention from marketers, who will be challenged to elevate their strategies to be less generic and more informed. Messaging will likely become more democratized, and we can expect to see minority engagement with national consumer brands increase significantly.

      So now that we’ve reached this tipping point in our nation’s history, how will your brand address it? We have some ideas if you aren’t quite sure where to begin.

      Nicole DeAngelis

      Interactive Producer
